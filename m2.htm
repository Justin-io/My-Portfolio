1. Prove that the number of vertices of odd degree in a graph is always even. (4 marks)

Answer:

This theorem is a direct consequence of the Handshaking Lemma, which is fundamental in graph theory. Let's break it down step by step:

  1. State the Handshaking Lemma: For any graph \( G = (V, E) \), the sum of the degrees of all vertices equals twice the number of edges: \[ \sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2|E| \]
  2. Reasoning: The right-hand side, \( 2|E| \), is clearly an even number since it's a multiple of 2. Therefore, the sum of all vertex degrees must also be even.
  3. Partition the vertices: Divide the vertex set \( V \) into two subsets:
    • \( O \): vertices with odd degree
    • \( E' \): vertices with even degree
  4. Express the sum: \[ \sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = \sum_{v \in O} \deg(v) + \sum_{v \in E'} \deg(v) \]
  5. Analyze parity:
    • The sum over \( E' \) (even degrees) is even (sum of even numbers).
    • For the total sum to be even, the sum over \( O \) must also be even.
  6. Key observation: The sum of an odd number of odd integers is odd. The sum of an even number of odd integers is even. Therefore, for \( \sum_{v \in O} \deg(v) \) to be even, the number of terms (\( |O| \)) must be even.
  7. Conclusion: Thus, the number of vertices with odd degree is always even.

Example: In a graph with degrees {3, 3, 2, 2, 4, 2}, the odd-degree vertices are those with degree 3 (there are 2 of them), which is even.

2. Define isomorphism between graphs. Check whether the given graphs are isomorphic? Justify your answer. (4 marks)

Answer:

Definition: Two graphs \( G_1 = (V_1, E_1) \) and \( G_2 = (V_2, E_2) \) are isomorphic if there exists a bijective function \( f: V_1 \to V_2 \) such that for any two vertices \( u, v \in V_1 \), \( u \) and \( v \) are adjacent in \( G_1 \) if and only if \( f(u) \) and \( f(v) \) are adjacent in \( G_2 \). This preserves both adjacency and non-adjacency.

Isomorphism Checking Protocol (when specific graphs are given):

  1. Quick checks:
    • Same number of vertices
    • Same number of edges
    • Same degree sequence (sorted list of vertex degrees)
  2. If above match, proceed to structural checks:
    • Same number of cycles of each length
    • Same connectivity (number of components)
    • Same diameter (longest shortest path)
    • Same girth (length of shortest cycle)
  3. Try to construct the bijection:
    • Start by mapping vertices with unique degree or unique neighborhood properties
    • Verify adjacency preservation
  4. Invariants to compare:
    • Adjacency matrix spectra (if advanced)
    • Presence of specific subgraphs (cliques, independent sets)
  5. If any invariant differs → graphs are not isomorphic
  6. If all invariants match → may be isomorphic (but not guaranteed)

Example justification: "Graphs G and H both have 6 vertices and 9 edges, with degree sequence (3,3,3,3,3,3). Both are regular of degree 3. However, G contains two triangles while H contains none. Since the number of triangles is an isomorphism invariant, G and H are not isomorphic."

4. If a graph has exactly two vertices of odd degree, prove that there must be a path joining these two vertices. (4 marks)

Answer:

Proof by contradiction and component analysis:

  1. Given: Graph \( G \) with exactly two vertices \( u \) and \( v \) having odd degree.
  2. Recall: By Question 1's theorem, the number of odd-degree vertices is even. Having exactly two satisfies this condition.
  3. Assume for contradiction that there is no path between \( u \) and \( v \).
  4. Implication: Then \( u \) and \( v \) belong to different connected components of \( G \).
  5. Consider the component containing \( u \): Call this component \( C_u \). Within \( C_u \), \( u \) has odd degree. By the Handshaking Lemma applied to \( C_u \):
    • Sum of degrees in \( C_u \) = \( 2|E(C_u)| \) (even)
    • But \( u \) contributes an odd number to this sum
    • Therefore, there must be at least one other vertex in \( C_u \) with odd degree to make the sum even
  6. Same logic for component containing \( v \): There must be at least one other odd-degree vertex in \( C_v \).
  7. Contradiction: Now we have at least:
    • 1 odd vertex (\( u \)) in \( C_u \)
    • At least 1 more odd vertex in \( C_u \)
    • 1 odd vertex (\( v \)) in \( C_v \)
    • At least 1 more odd vertex in \( C_v \)
    That's at least 4 odd-degree vertices, contradicting "exactly two."
  8. Conclusion: The assumption that \( u \) and \( v \) are in different components must be false. Therefore, \( u \) and \( v \) are in the same connected component, meaning a path exists between them.

Alternative constructive proof: Consider removing all components with no odd vertices. What remains must have the two odd vertices connected through a trail.

10. Prove that a graph G is disconnected if and only if its vertex set V can be partitioned into two non-empty disjoint subsets V1 and V2 such that there exists no edge in G whose one end vertex is in V1 and the other in V2. (5 marks)

Answer:

This is a fundamental characterization of disconnected graphs. Let's prove both directions thoroughly:

Part 1 (⇒): If G is disconnected, then such a partition exists

  1. Definition: A graph is disconnected if it has at least two connected components.
  2. Construction: Let \( C_1 \) be any connected component of \( G \). Define:
    • \( V_1 = V(C_1) \) (vertices of component \( C_1 \))
    • \( V_2 = V \setminus V_1 \) (all other vertices)
  3. Properties:
    • \( V_1 \) and \( V_2 \) are non-empty (since \( C_1 \) is a proper component and G is disconnected)
    • \( V_1 \cap V_2 = \emptyset \) (by construction)
    • \( V_1 \cup V_2 = V \) (by construction)
  4. No edges between \( V_1 \) and \( V_2 \): By definition of connected component, there are no edges between vertices in different components. If there were an edge with one endpoint in \( V_1 \) and the other in \( V_2 \), then that edge would connect \( C_1 \) to another component, contradicting the definition of components.
  5. Thus the partition exists.

Part 2 (⇐): If such a partition exists, then G is disconnected

  1. Given: Partition \( V = V_1 \cup V_2 \) with \( V_1, V_2 \) non-empty, disjoint, and no edges between them.
  2. Take any vertex \( u \in V_1 \) and \( v \in V_2 \).
  3. Claim: There is no path from \( u \) to \( v \).
  4. Proof of claim: Suppose a path \( u = x_0, x_1, x_2, \dots, x_k = v \) exists. Since \( u \in V_1 \) and \( v \in V_2 \), there must be some index \( i \) where \( x_i \in V_1 \) and \( x_{i+1} \in V_2 \) (or vice versa). But then the edge \( x_i x_{i+1} \) connects \( V_1 \) to \( V_2 \), contradicting the "no edges" condition.
  5. Since there exist vertices with no path between them, the graph is disconnected by definition.

Conclusion: The equivalence is proved.

Corollary: This shows that connectivity is equivalent to having no such "disconnecting partition" of vertices.

11. The maximum number of edges in a simple graph with n vertices is \( \frac{n(n-1)}{2} \). (5 marks)

Answer:

Comprehensive proof with multiple approaches:

Approach 1: Combinatorial Counting

  1. In a simple graph (no loops, no multiple edges), each edge is uniquely determined by an unordered pair of distinct vertices.
  2. From \( n \) vertices, the number of ways to choose 2 distinct vertices is the binomial coefficient: \[ \binom{n}{2} = \frac{n!}{2!(n-2)!} = \frac{n(n-1)}{2} \]
  3. Each such pair can have either 0 or 1 edge in a simple graph.
  4. To maximize edges, we put an edge for every possible pair, resulting in the complete graph \( K_n \).
  5. Therefore, maximum edges = \( \binom{n}{2} = \frac{n(n-1)}{2} \).

Approach 2: Sum of Degrees

  1. In any simple graph, maximum degree of a vertex is \( n-1 \) (connected to all other vertices).
  2. If we try to maximize edges, we should maximize each vertex's degree.
  3. In the complete graph \( K_n \), each vertex has degree \( n-1 \).
  4. Sum of all degrees = \( n(n-1) \).
  5. By Handshaking Lemma: \( \sum \deg(v) = 2|E| \)
  6. So \( 2|E| = n(n-1) \Rightarrow |E| = \frac{n(n-1)}{2} \).

Approach 3: Induction

  1. Base case: \( n=1 \): Max edges = 0 = \( \frac{1×0}{2} \) ✓ \( n=2 \): Max edges = 1 = \( \frac{2×1}{2} \) ✓
  2. Inductive hypothesis: Assume true for \( n = k \): max edges = \( \frac{k(k-1)}{2} \).
  3. For \( n = k+1 \): Add a new vertex to the complete graph on \( k \) vertices. To make it complete on \( k+1 \) vertices, connect the new vertex to all \( k \) existing vertices.
  4. New edge count = \( \frac{k(k-1)}{2} + k = \frac{k(k-1) + 2k}{2} = \frac{k(k+1)}{2} = \frac{(k+1)k}{2} \).
  5. By induction, formula holds for all \( n \).

Why this is maximum: Any graph with \( n \) vertices that is not complete has at least one missing edge between some pair of vertices, thus has fewer than \( \binom{n}{2} \) edges.

Example: For \( n=4 \), maximum edges = \( \frac{4×3}{2} = 6 \), realized by \( K_4 \).

12. Prove that a simple graph with n vertices and k components can have at most \( \frac{(n-k)(n-k+1)}{2} \) edges. (4 marks)

Answer:

This is an important result about maximizing edges in a graph with given number of components.

Proof strategy: To maximize edges with fixed \( n \) and \( k \), make \( k-1 \) components as small as possible (isolated vertices) and one component as large as possible (complete graph).

Step-by-step proof:

  1. Let the component sizes be \( n_1, n_2, \dots, n_k \), where \( \sum_{i=1}^k n_i = n \) and each \( n_i \geq 1 \).
  2. Maximum edges in component with \( n_i \) vertices = \( \frac{n_i(n_i-1)}{2} \) (if it's complete).
  3. Total maximum edges = \( \sum_{i=1}^k \frac{n_i(n_i-1)}{2} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \sum n_i^2 - \sum n_i \right) = \frac{1}{2} \left( \sum n_i^2 - n \right) \).
  4. To maximize \( \sum n_i^2 \) given \( \sum n_i = n \) and each \( n_i \geq 1 \), we make one \( n_i \) as large as possible and others as small as possible.
  5. The smallest possible size for \( k-1 \) components is 1 (isolated vertices).
  6. Thus, optimal configuration:
    • \( k-1 \) components of size 1
    • 1 component of size \( n - (k-1) = n-k+1 \)
  7. Maximum edges = \( \frac{(n-k+1)(n-k)}{2} \) (from the large complete component) + 0 (from isolated vertices).
  8. So maximum = \( \frac{(n-k+1)(n-k)}{2} \).

Alternative justification: - Adding an edge between two components reduces number of components by 1. - To keep \( k \) components fixed, we cannot connect different components. - Within a component of size \( m \), maximum edges is \( \binom{m}{2} \). - Sum is maximized when one component is huge and others are tiny.

Example: \( n=7, k=3 \): Optimal: Component sizes: 1, 1, 5 Max edges = \( \frac{5×4}{2} = 10 \) Check: Complete graph on 5 vertices has 10 edges, plus 2 isolated vertices.

18. Explain the operations union, intersection, and ring sum on graph. Find the union, intersection, and ring sum of below graphs. (5 marks)

Answer:

Detailed definitions with formal notation:

Let \( G_1 = (V_1, E_1) \) and \( G_2 = (V_2, E_2) \) be two graphs.

  1. Union (\( G_1 \cup G_2 \)):
    • Vertex set: \( V_1 \cup V_2 \)
    • Edge set: \( E_1 \cup E_2 \)
    • Properties: Contains all vertices and edges from both graphs. Multiple edges may occur if both graphs have the same edge.
    • Formally: \( G_1 \cup G_2 = (V_1 \cup V_2, E_1 \cup E_2) \)
  2. Intersection (\( G_1 \cap G_2 \)):
    • Vertex set: \( V_1 \cap V_2 \) (only common vertices)
    • Edge set: \( E_1 \cap E_2 \) (only edges present in both graphs with same endpoints)
    • Properties: Represents the common subgraph. May be disconnected.
    • Formally: \( G_1 \cap G_2 = (V_1 \cap V_2, E_1 \cap E_2) \)
  3. Ring Sum (\( G_1 \oplus G_2 \)):
    • Vertex set: \( V_1 \cup V_2 \)
    • Edge set: \( (E_1 \cup E_2) - (E_1 \cap E_2) \) = edges in exactly one of the graphs
    • Properties: Symmetric difference of edge sets. Removes common edges.
    • Alternative definition: \( G_1 \oplus G_2 = (G_1 \cup G_2) - (G_1 \cap G_2) \)
    • Note: This operation is commutative and associative.

Example with specific graphs: Let \( G_1 \): V={a,b,c}, E={ab, bc} Let \( G_2 \): V={b,c,d}, E={bc, cd}

  • Union \( G_1 \cup G_2 \): V={a,b,c,d}, E={ab, bc, cd} (Visual: Path a-b-c-d)
  • Intersection \( G_1 \cap G_2 \): V={b,c}, E={bc} (Visual: Just edge bc)
  • Ring sum \( G_1 \oplus G_2 \): V={a,b,c,d}, E={ab, cd} (Visual: Disjoint edges ab and cd)

Geometric interpretation: Union overlays graphs, intersection finds common part, ring sum finds "exclusive or" of edges.

Applications: Ring sum is used in circuit theory, network analysis, and cycle space calculations.

19. Prove that a connected graph G is an Euler graph if and only if all the vertices of G are of even degree. (5 marks)

Answer:

This is Euler's Theorem, one of the most famous results in graph theory.

Definitions first: - Euler graph: Contains an Eulerian circuit (closed trail that uses every edge exactly once and returns to starting vertex). - Eulerian trail: Uses every edge exactly once but may not return to start (open). - Even degree: Degree is divisible by 2.

Proof in two parts:

Part 1 (⇒): If G has an Euler circuit, then all vertices have even degree

  1. Let \( C = v_0 e_1 v_1 e_2 v_2 \dots e_m v_0 \) be an Euler circuit (length m = |E|).
  2. Observation: Each time the circuit visits a vertex (other than the starting point at the beginning/end), it arrives via one edge and departs via another.
  3. Counting contributions: For each vertex \( v \):
    • If \( v \) is not the starting vertex: Each visit contributes 2 to deg(v) (one in, one out).
    • If \( v \) is the starting vertex \( v_0 \): The circuit starts here and ends here. The initial departure contributes 1, and the final arrival contributes 1, totaling 2.
  4. Conclusion: Every vertex has degree = 2 × (number of times circuit visits that vertex), which is even.

Part 2 (⇐): If G is connected and all vertices have even degree, then G has an Euler circuit

Proof by construction (Hierholzer's algorithm):

  1. Step 1 - Start with any vertex: Since all degrees are even and G is connected, every vertex has degree at least 2 (except trivial case). Start at any vertex \( v_0 \).
  2. Step 2 - Build initial cycle: Follow edges without repeating any, until returning to \( v_0 \). This is possible because:
    • Whenever we enter a vertex (other than start), we can always leave via another unused edge (since degree is even, and used edges come in pairs).
    • Eventually we must return to \( v_0 \) because the graph is finite.
  3. Step 3 - Check if all edges used: If the cycle uses all edges, we're done (it's an Euler circuit).
  4. Step 4 - Otherwise, find unused edges: Let \( C \) be the current cycle. Since G is connected, there must be some vertex \( u \) on \( C \) that has unused incident edges.
  5. Step 5 - Build another cycle: Starting at \( u \), use only unused edges to create another cycle \( C' \) that returns to \( u \). This is possible by same reasoning as Step 2.
  6. Step 6 - Merge cycles: Splice \( C' \) into \( C \) at vertex \( u \). This creates a longer cycle.
  7. Step 7 - Repeat: Continue until all edges are used.
  8. Termination: Since graph is finite and each step uses more edges, the process terminates with an Euler circuit.

Why connectivity is necessary: Without connectivity, each component could satisfy the even-degree condition separately, but there would be no single circuit covering all edges of the whole graph.

Example: A cycle graph \( C_n \) (all vertices degree 2) has an Euler circuit. A "bowtie" of two triangles sharing a vertex (degrees: 2,2,2,2,2,2) also has an Euler circuit.

Historical context: Euler solved the Königsberg bridge problem (1736), which led to this theorem. The "only if" direction is easier; the "if" direction requires the constructive proof above.

22. Draw a graph which is Eulerian but not Hamiltonian. (5 marks)

Answer:

Concept clarification: - Eulerian: Has Euler circuit (closed trail covering all edges once) - Hamiltonian: Has Hamiltonian cycle (cycle visiting all vertices once) - These are independent properties: A graph can be one, both, or neither.

Construction of Eulerian but not Hamiltonian graph:

Standard example: The "House of Santa Claus" or certain hierarchical star-like graphs.

Better known example: Take two copies of \( K_5 \) (complete graph on 5 vertices) and join them at a single vertex.

Detailed construction:

  1. Start with two disjoint complete graphs \( K_5 \), call them \( A \) and \( B \).
  2. Identify one vertex from \( A \) with one vertex from \( B \) (merge them into a single vertex \( v \)).
  3. Resulting graph \( G \):
    • Total vertices: \( 5 + 5 - 1 = 9 \)
    • Vertex \( v \) has degree: \( 4 \) (from A) + \( 4 \) (from B) = \( 8 \)
    • Other vertices: degree 4 (within their own \( K_5 \)) + possibly connection to \( v \)

Why it's Eulerian: - All vertices have even degree (4 or 8) - Graph is connected - By Euler's theorem, it has an Euler circuit

Why it's not Hamiltonian: - Consider what a Hamiltonian cycle would require - The cycle must visit all vertices exactly once - Vertex \( v \) is a cut vertex (removing it disconnects the graph into two components: A\{v} and B\{v}) - In any Hamiltonian cycle, when we first enter component A\{v} from \( v \), we must eventually leave it to visit vertices in B\{v} - But to leave A\{v}, we must go through \( v \) again - This means \( v \) would be visited more than once, violating Hamiltonian condition - Formally: Any cycle containing all vertices would have to pass through \( v \) at least twice to visit both sides, impossible in a Hamiltonian cycle

Simpler well-known example: The "Butterfly graph" or two triangles sharing exactly one vertex: - Vertices: {a,b,c,d,e} where {a,b,c} form triangle, {a,d,e} form another triangle sharing vertex a - Degrees: a:4, b:2, c:2, d:2, e:2 (all even → Eulerian) - Not Hamiltonian: Vertex a is cut vertex, same argument as above

(But this actually might be Hamiltonian... Let's use a safer example)

Actually proven example: Certain "Grötzsch graph" variants or specifically constructed graphs where removing certain vertices creates more components than vertices removed.

Known fact: All Hamiltonian graphs are 2-connected (no cut vertices), but Eulerian graphs can have cut vertices. So any Eulerian graph with a cut vertex cannot be Hamiltonian (unless trivial).

Best classroom example: A graph shaped like the letter "θ" (theta) or two cycles joined at a single vertex.

23. Define Eulerian graph and Hamilton graphs with examples. (5 marks)

Answer:

Eulerian Graph:

  • Definition: A connected graph that contains an Eulerian circuit (also called Euler circuit). An Eulerian circuit is a closed walk that traverses every edge exactly once and returns to the starting vertex.
  • Alternative: A graph is Eulerian if it has a closed trail containing all edges.
  • Characterization (Euler's Theorem): A connected graph is Eulerian iff every vertex has even degree.
  • Examples:
    1. Cycle graph \( C_n \): Every vertex degree 2, has obvious Euler circuit going around the cycle.
    2. Complete graph \( K_n \) with odd n: For odd n ≥ 3, every vertex has degree n-1 (even), so Eulerian. \( K_3 \) (triangle) is Eulerian.
    3. Any connected graph where all vertices have even degree.
  • Non-example: \( K_4 \) has vertices of degree 3 (odd), so not Eulerian (but it has Eulerian trails).

Hamiltonian Graph:

  • Definition: A graph that contains a Hamiltonian cycle. A Hamiltonian cycle is a cycle that visits each vertex exactly once (except the starting/ending vertex, visited twice).
  • Alternative: Contains a spanning cycle.
  • No simple characterization: Unlike Eulerian graphs, there's no known simple necessary and sufficient condition for Hamiltonian graphs. This is an NP-complete problem.
  • Sufficient conditions (not necessary):
    • Dirac's Theorem: If G has n ≥ 3 vertices and every vertex has degree ≥ n/2, then G is Hamiltonian.
    • Ore's Theorem: If for every pair of non-adjacent vertices, sum of degrees ≥ n, then G is Hamiltonian.
  • Examples:
    1. Cycle graph \( C_n \): Trivially Hamiltonian (the cycle itself).
    2. Complete graph \( K_n \) (n ≥ 3): Always Hamiltonian (many Hamiltonian cycles).
    3. Platonic solids: Their skeletons are Hamiltonian (can trace along edges visiting each vertex once).
  • Non-example: A tree with more than 2 vertices is never Hamiltonian (contains no cycles at all).

Comparison:

Property Eulerian Hamiltonian
Focus Edges Vertices
Characterization Simple: all degrees even No simple characterization
Complexity Polynomial time to check NP-complete to check
Example of both \( K_3 \) (triangle) \( K_3 \)
Eulerian but not Hamiltonian Two \( K_5 \) joined at one vertex See Q22
Hamiltonian but not Eulerian \( K_4 \) (all degrees 3, odd) \( K_4 \)

Historical notes: - Eulerian: Solved by Leonhard Euler in 1736 (Königsberg bridges). - Hamiltonian: Named after William Rowan Hamilton who studied them in 1850s, though the problem was known earlier.

25. State travelling salesman problem. How it is related to Hamiltonian circuits. (5 marks)

Answer:

Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP):

  • Statement: Given a list of cities and the distances (or costs) between each pair of cities, find the shortest possible route that visits each city exactly once and returns to the origin city.
  • Formal definition: Given a complete weighted graph \( K_n \) with edge weights \( w(e) \), find a Hamiltonian cycle of minimum total weight.
  • Variants:
    1. Metric TSP: Distances satisfy triangle inequality.
    2. Euclidean TSP: Cities are points in plane with Euclidean distances.
    3. Asymmetric TSP: Distance from A to B ≠ distance from B to A.
  • Computational complexity: NP-hard (no known polynomial-time solution for large n).

Relationship to Hamiltonian cycles:

  1. Fundamental equivalence: The decision version of TSP is directly reducible to the Hamiltonian cycle problem:
    • TSP Decision Problem: "Is there a tour of length ≤ L?"
    • Reduction: Create an unweighted graph where edge exists iff distance ≤ some threshold. TSP solution exists iff Hamiltonian cycle exists.
  2. TSP as weighted Hamiltonian cycle problem:
    • Consider a complete graph \( K_n \) with vertices as cities.
    • Each edge has weight = distance between corresponding cities.
    • A Hamiltonian cycle in this graph corresponds to a permutation of cities (tour).
    • The TSP seeks the Hamiltonian cycle with minimum sum of edge weights.
  3. If distances satisfy triangle inequality:
    • Minimum spanning tree gives 2-approximation for TSP.
    • Christofides algorithm gives 1.5-approximation.
    • But exact solution still requires finding optimal Hamiltonian cycle.
  4. If all distances are 1:
    • TSP reduces to: "Does the graph have a Hamiltonian cycle?"
    • Then TSP solution exists iff graph is Hamiltonian, and optimal tour length = n.
  5. Complexity connection:
    • Hamiltonian Cycle Problem is NP-complete.
    • TSP is NP-hard (at least as hard).
    • In fact, TSP contains Hamiltonian Cycle as a special case.

Mathematical formulation: Let \( d_{ij} \) = distance from city i to city j. Let \( x_{ij} = 1 \) if tour goes directly from i to j, else 0. Minimize: \( \sum_{i,j} d_{ij} x_{ij} \) Subject to: - Each city entered exactly once: \( \sum_i x_{ij} = 1 \) for all j - Each city left exactly once: \( \sum_j x_{ij} = 1 \) for all i - No subtours (enforces single cycle visiting all cities)

Practical significance: - Logistics and delivery routes - Circuit board drilling - DNA sequencing - Astronomy (telescope observation scheduling)

Example: 4 cities: A, B, C, D with distances: AB=10, AC=15, AD=20, BC=35, BD=25, CD=30 Possible tours: A-B-C-D-A: 10+35+30+20 = 95 A-B-D-C-A: 10+25+30+15 = 80 (better) A-C-B-D-A: 15+35+25+20 = 95 etc. TSP finds the minimum.

Without Hamiltonian cycle, there's no tour visiting all cities exactly once, so TSP has no solution (unless we allow revisiting, which becomes different problem).

26. State and prove Euler's theorem. (5 marks)

Answer:

Euler's Theorem for planar graphs (also called Euler's Polyhedron Formula):

Statement: For any connected planar graph (or polyhedron) drawn in the plane without edge intersections: \[ V - E + F = 2 \] where: - \( V \) = number of vertices - \( E \) = number of edges - \( F \) = number of faces (including the unbounded outer face)

Proof by induction on number of edges:

Base cases:

  1. Tree (acyclic connected graph):
    • For a tree: \( E = V - 1 \)
    • A tree has only one face (the outer face), so \( F = 1 \)
    • Check: \( V - (V-1) + 1 = 2 \) ✓
  2. Minimal non-tree: Simple cycle (polygon):
    • \( V = E \) (cycle has same number of vertices and edges)
    • Creates 2 faces: inside and outside, so \( F = 2 \)
    • Check: \( V - V + 2 = 2 \) ✓

Inductive hypothesis: Assume formula holds for all connected planar graphs with fewer than \( m \) edges.

Inductive step: Consider connected planar graph \( G \) with \( E = m \) edges (\( m \geq 1 \)).

Case 1: If \( G \) has a cycle:

  • Remove one edge from a cycle. Call new graph \( G' \).
  • Properties of \( G' \):
    • Still connected (edge from cycle removal doesn't disconnect)
    • \( V' = V \)
    • \( E' = E - 1 = m - 1 \)
    • \( F' = F - 1 \) (removing edge merges two faces)
  • By inductive hypothesis: \( V' - E' + F' = 2 \)
  • Substitute: \( V - (E-1) + (F-1) = V - E + F = 2 \)
  • So formula holds for \( G \).

Case 2: If \( G \) has no cycle (i.e., \( G \) is a tree):

  • Already covered in base case.

Thus by induction, formula holds for all connected planar graphs.

Alternative proof outline (by face induction):

  1. Start with spanning tree of \( G \) (has \( V-1 \) edges, 1 face).
  2. Formula holds for tree: \( V - (V-1) + 1 = 2 \).
  3. Add back remaining edges one by one.
  4. Each added edge:
    • Increases \( E \) by 1
    • Splits a face into two, increasing \( F \) by 1
    • Net change: \( (V - (E+1) + (F+1)) = V - E + F \) (unchanged)
  5. So invariant \( V - E + F = 2 \) maintained.

For graphs with \( k \) components: More general formula: \[ V - E + F = 1 + k \] (Proof similar, using induction on number of edges or components.)

Applications:

  1. Proving \( K_5 \) and \( K_{3,3} \) are non-planar:
    • For \( K_5 \): \( V=5, E=10 \). If planar, \( F = 2 - V + E = 7 \).
    • But each face has ≥3 edges, and each edge borders 2 faces, so \( 2E \geq 3F \Rightarrow 20 \geq 21 \), contradiction.
  2. For \( K_{3,3} \): \( V=6, E=9 \), if planar \( F=5 \).
    • Bipartite → no odd cycles → each face has ≥4 edges
    • So \( 2E \geq 4F \Rightarrow 18 \geq 20 \), contradiction.
  3. Upper bound on edges in planar graph: \( E \leq 3V - 6 \) (if \( V \geq 3 \)). Proof: \( 2E \geq 3F \) and \( F = 2 - V + E \Rightarrow 2E \geq 3(2 - V + E) \Rightarrow E \leq 3V - 6 \).

Historical note: Euler stated this for polyhedra in 1758, but first complete proof for planar graphs was given by Cauchy in 1813.
