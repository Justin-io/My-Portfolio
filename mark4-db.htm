I. FOUNDATIONAL PHYSICS

Topic: Classical Mechanics – Lagrangian & Hamiltonian Formalisms, Poisson Brackets, Phase Space, Symmetries, and Noether’s Theorem

Comprehensive Study Notes – Depth-Oriented, Structured for Mastery

1. Classical Mechanics: Beyond Newton

1.1 Limitations of Newtonian Mechanics

  • Newton’s laws (F = ma) are vectorial, requiring explicit force resolution in Cartesian or other coordinate systems.
  • They become cumbersome for:
    • Systems with constraints (e.g., pendulum, bead on a wire)
    • Many-body problems with holonomic constraints
    • Systems where forces are unknown or internal (e.g., rigid bodies)
  • Coordinate dependence: Forces and accelerations must be recomputed when changing coordinates.
Key insight: Newtonian mechanics is not manifestly covariant under general coordinate transformations.

1.2 The Principle of Least Action

  • All fundamental laws of physics (classical, quantum, relativistic) can be derived from variational principles.
  • Action \( S \) is a functional of the path \( q(t) \):
    \[ S[q(t)] = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(q, \dot{q}, t) \, dt \]
  • Hamilton’s Principle: The physical path between two fixed endpoints \( q(t_1) \) and \( q(t_2) \) is the one that makes the action stationary (δS = 0).
  • This yields the Euler–Lagrange equations.

2. Lagrangian Mechanics

2.1 Definition of the Lagrangian

  • For conservative systems:
    \[ L(q, \dot{q}, t) = T - V \]
    where \( T \) = kinetic energy, \( V \) = potential energy.
  • Generalized coordinates \( q_i \) (i = 1…n) replace Cartesian coordinates; they incorporate constraints automatically.
  • Degrees of freedom: Number of independent generalized coordinates.

2.2 Euler–Lagrange Equations

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i} = 0 \quad \text{(for each } i\text{)} \]
  • Derivation: Apply δS = 0 with fixed endpoints → integration by parts → fundamental lemma of calculus of variations.
  • Advantages:
    • Scalar formulation (no vectors)
    • Automatically handles constraints (if holonomic)
    • Covariant under coordinate transformations: if \( q \to Q(q) \), equations retain form.

2.3 Examples

  • Simple pendulum: \( q = \theta \), \( T = \frac{1}{2} m l^2 \dot{\theta}^2 \), \( V = -mgl \cos\theta \) → \( L = \frac{1}{2} m l^2 \dot{\theta}^2 + mgl \cos\theta \)
  • Double pendulum: 2 DOF → 2 coupled nonlinear E-L equations.
  • Charged particle in EM field: \( L = \frac{1}{2} m \dot{\mathbf{r}}^2 - q\phi + q \dot{\mathbf{r}} \cdot \mathbf{A} \)

2.4 Non-conservative Forces

  • Can be included via generalized forces \( Q_i \):
    \[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i} = Q_i \]
  • Rayleigh dissipation function \( \mathcal{F} = \frac{1}{2} \sum c_{ij} \dot{q}_i \dot{q}_j \) for linear damping: \( Q_i = -\frac{\partial \mathcal{F}}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \)

3. Symmetries and Conservation Laws → Noether’s Theorem

3.1 What is a Symmetry?

  • A transformation of coordinates/time that leaves the action invariant (up to a boundary term).
  • Types:
    • Continuous global symmetries: e.g., translation, rotation, time shift.
    • Discrete symmetries: e.g., parity, time reversal (Noether’s theorem applies only to continuous symmetries).

3.2 Noether’s Theorem (1918)

Statement: For every continuous differentiable symmetry of the action, there exists a conserved current (and hence a conserved charge).
  • Mathematical form: Suppose under an infinitesimal transformation:
    \[ q_i(t) \to q_i(t) + \varepsilon \delta q_i(t) \]
    the Lagrangian changes by a total time derivative:
    \[ \delta L = \varepsilon \frac{dF}{dt} \]
    Then the quantity
    \[ Q = \sum_i \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \delta q_i - F \]
    is conserved: \( \frac{dQ}{dt} = 0 \) on-shell (i.e., when E-L equations hold).

3.3 Key Examples

Symmetry Transformation Conserved Quantity
Time translation\( t \to t + \varepsilon \)Energy \( H = \sum p_i \dot{q}_i - L \)
Spatial translation\( \mathbf{r} \to \mathbf{r} + \varepsilon \hat{x} \)Linear momentum \( p_x \)
Rotation\( \mathbf{r} \to \mathbf{r} + \varepsilon (\hat{n} \times \mathbf{r}) \)Angular momentum \( \mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p} \)
Phase rotation (complex field)\( \psi \to e^{i\varepsilon} \psi \)Charge (in field theory)

Deep implication: Conservation laws are not coincidental—they reflect the geometry of spacetime and internal symmetries.

4. Hamiltonian Mechanics

4.1 From Lagrangian to Hamiltonian

  • Define generalized momenta:
    \[ p_i = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \]
  • Assume the map \( \dot{q} \mapsto p \) is invertible (regular Lagrangian).
  • Perform Legendre transform:
    \[ H(q, p, t) = \sum_i p_i \dot{q}_i - L(q, \dot{q}, t) \]
    where \( \dot{q} \) is expressed in terms of \( p \).

4.2 Hamilton’s Equations

\[ \dot{q}_i = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}, \quad \dot{p}_i = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i} \]
  • 2n first-order ODEs (vs. n second-order in Lagrangian)
  • Phase space: 2n-dimensional space with coordinates \( (q_1, ..., q_n, p_1, ..., p_n) \)

4.3 Physical Interpretation

  • For natural systems (\( L = T - V \), \( T \) quadratic in \( \dot{q} \)):
    \[ H = T + V = \text{Total energy} \]
  • But not always: if \( L \) has explicit time dependence or velocity-dependent potentials (e.g., EM), \( H \) may not equal energy.

4.4 Advantages of Hamiltonian Formulation

  • Reveals symplectic structure of mechanics
  • Foundation for statistical mechanics (Liouville’s theorem)
  • Natural bridge to quantum mechanics (canonical quantization)
  • Enables canonical transformations and action-angle variables

5. Phase Space and Poisson Brackets

5.1 Phase Space Geometry

  • Each point \( (q, p) \) represents a complete state of the system.
  • Time evolution = trajectory in phase space.
  • Liouville’s Theorem: Phase space volume is conserved under Hamiltonian flow (incompressible fluid analogy).

5.2 Poisson Brackets

  • For any two functions \( f(q, p, t) \), \( g(q, p, t) \):
    \[ \{f, g\} = \sum_i \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial q_i} \frac{\partial g}{\partial p_i} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i} \frac{\partial g}{\partial q_i} \right) \]
  • Fundamental brackets:
    \[ \{q_i, q_j\} = 0, \quad \{p_i, p_j\} = 0, \quad \{q_i, p_j\} = \delta_{ij} \]
  • Time evolution:
    \[ \frac{df}{dt} = \{f, H\} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} \]
    → In particular, Hamilton’s equations are \( \dot{q}_i = \{q_i, H\} \), \( \dot{p}_i = \{p_i, H\} \)

5.3 Role in Symmetries and Conservation

  • A quantity \( Q \) is conserved iff \( \{Q, H\} = 0 \) (and \( \partial Q/\partial t = 0 \))
  • Infinitesimal canonical transformation generated by \( G \): \( \delta f = \varepsilon \{f, G\} \)
  • Noether charges generate symmetry transformations via Poisson brackets.

5.4 Algebraic Structure

  • Poisson brackets satisfy:
    • Antisymmetry: \( \{f, g\} = -\{g, f\} \)
    • Jacobi identity: \( \{f, \{g, h\}\} + \{g, \{h, f\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\} = 0 \)
    • Leibniz rule: \( \{fg, h\} = f\{g, h\} + \{f, h\}g \)
  • This makes phase space a Poisson manifold.

6. Canonical Transformations & Symplectic Structure

6.1 Goal

Find new coordinates \( (Q, P) \) such that Hamilton’s equations retain form.

  • Condition: \( \{Q_i, Q_j\}_{q,p} = 0 \), \( \{P_i, P_j\}_{q,p} = 0 \), \( \{Q_i, P_j\}_{q,p} = \delta_{ij} \)
  • Equivalent to preserving the symplectic 2-form:
    \[ \omega = \sum_i dq_i \wedge dp_i \]
  • Symplectic matrix \( J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I \\ -I & 0 \end{pmatrix} \); transformation matrix \( M \) must satisfy \( M^T J M = J \)

6.2 Generating Functions

  • Four types (e.g., \( F_1(q, Q, t) \)) relate old and new variables via partial derivatives.
  • Enable systematic solution of dynamics (e.g., Hamilton–Jacobi theory).

8. Summary: Conceptual Hierarchy

  1. Newton: Forces → trajectories (vectorial, coordinate-dependent)
  2. Lagrange: Action principle → E-L equations (scalar, covariant, handles constraints)
  3. Noether: Symmetries ⇄ Conservation laws (deep physical insight)
  4. Hamilton: Phase space + Poisson brackets → geometric, algebraic structure
  5. Symplectic geometry: Unifying framework for classical and quantum dynamics

9. Common Pitfalls & Clarifications

  • Myth: “Lagrangian is always T – V” → Only for conservative, holonomic systems in inertial frames. EM, relativistic, or dissipative systems require generalized forms.
  • Myth: “Hamiltonian is always total energy” → False if \( L \) has explicit time dependence or non-standard kinetic terms.
  • Caution: Not all symmetries lead to conservation if constraints are non-holonomic or if symmetry is quasi-symmetry (δL = dF/dt ≠ 0).
  • Note: Noether’s theorem applies to global continuous symmetries. Gauge symmetries lead to constraints (Dirac theory), not conserved charges.

10. Advanced Extensions (For Further Study)

  • Hamilton–Jacobi equation: \( H(q, \partial S/\partial q, t) + \partial S/\partial t = 0 \)
  • Action-angle variables: For integrable systems
  • Dirac brackets: For constrained Hamiltonian systems
  • Geometric mechanics: On manifolds, fiber bundles
  • Classical field theory: Lagrangian density \( \mathcal{L}(\phi, \partial_\mu \phi) \), Noether currents \( j^\mu \)

II. FOUNDATIONAL PHYSICS

Topic: Electromagnetism – Maxwell’s Equations in Covariant Form, Gauge Invariance, and Retarded Potentials

Comprehensive, Depth-Oriented Study Notes – Structured for Conceptual Mastery and Technical Fluency

1. Classical Electromagnetism: A Relativistic Theory

Key insight: Electromagnetism was already a relativistic theory before Einstein—Maxwell’s equations implied special relativity.
  • Pre-relativity EM used 3-vector formalism (E, B fields), obscuring spacetime symmetry.
  • Covariant formulation (4D spacetime) reveals:
    • Unified electric and magnetic fields
    • Natural incorporation of Lorentz invariance
    • Elegant expression of conservation laws
    • Foundation for quantum electrodynamics (QED)

2. Spacetime Framework: Minkowski Space

  • Metric signature: We adopt \( (+, -, -, -) \) → \( \eta_{\mu\nu} = \text{diag}(1, -1, -1, -1) \)
  • 4-position: \( x^\mu = (ct, x, y, z) \)
  • 4-gradient: \( \partial_\mu = \left( \frac{1}{c}\frac{\partial}{\partial t}, \nabla \right) \), \( \partial^\mu = \left( \frac{1}{c}\frac{\partial}{\partial t}, -\nabla \right) \)
  • d’Alembertian: \( \Box = \partial^\mu \partial_\mu = \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2} - \nabla^2 \)

3. Electromagnetic Field Tensor \( F^{\mu\nu} \)

3.1 Construction from Potentials

  • 4-potential: \( A^\mu = (\phi/c, \mathbf{A}) \), where:
    • \( \phi \): scalar electric potential
    • \( \mathbf{A} \): vector magnetic potential
  • Field tensor (antisymmetric, rank-2):
    \[ F^{\mu\nu} = \partial^\mu A^\nu - \partial^\nu A^\mu \]

3.2 Explicit Matrix Form

\[ F^{\mu\nu} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -E_x/c & -E_y/c & -E_z/c \\ E_x/c & 0 & -B_z & B_y \\ E_y/c & B_z & 0 & -B_x \\ E_z/c & -B_y & B_x & 0 \end{pmatrix} \]
  • Dual tensor (for magnetic sources, if any):
    \[ \tilde{F}^{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} F_{\rho\sigma} \]

3.3 Invariants of the EM Field

Two Lorentz scalars:

  1. \( F^{\mu\nu} F_{\mu\nu} = 2\left(B^2 - \frac{E^2}{c^2}\right) \)
  2. \( F^{\mu\nu} \tilde{F}_{\mu\nu} = -\frac{4}{c} \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{B} \)

→ These are the only independent scalar invariants of EM.

4. Maxwell’s Equations in Covariant Form

4.1 Homogeneous Equations (Bianchi Identity)

\[ \partial_\lambda F_{\mu\nu} + \partial_\mu F_{\nu\lambda} + \partial_\nu F_{\lambda\mu} = 0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \partial_{[\alpha} F_{\beta\gamma]} = 0 \]
  • Equivalent to:
    • \( \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0 \) (no magnetic monopoles)
    • \( \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \) (Faraday’s law)
Geometric meaning: \( F = dA \) is an exact 2-form → automatically closed (\( dF = 0 \)).

4.2 Inhomogeneous Equations (Dynamical)

Define 4-current: \( J^\mu = (c\rho, \mathbf{J}) \)

\[ \partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 J^\nu \]
  • Expands to:
    • \( \nu = 0 \): \( \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho / \varepsilon_0 \) (Gauss’s law)
    • \( \nu = i \): \( \nabla \times \mathbf{B} - \frac{1}{c^2} \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} \) (Ampère–Maxwell law)

4.3 Compact Summary

\[ \boxed{ \begin{aligned} \partial_{[\alpha} F_{\beta\gamma]} &= 0 \\ \partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} &= \mu_0 J^\nu \end{aligned} } \]

Manifest Lorentz covariance: All inertial observers agree on form.

5. Gauge Invariance

5.1 Definition

  • The physical fields \( \mathbf{E}, \mathbf{B} \) are unchanged under:
    \[ A^\mu \to A^\mu + \partial^\mu \chi \]
    for any smooth scalar function \( \chi(x^\mu) \).
  • Since \( F^{\mu\nu} = \partial^\mu A^\nu - \partial^\nu A^\mu \), the derivative of \( \chi \) cancels.

5.2 Physical Significance

  • Redundancy in description: \( A^\mu \) has 4 components, but only 2 physical degrees of freedom (photon polarizations).
  • Constraint: Gauge freedom allows us to impose gauge conditions to simplify equations.

5.3 Common Gauges

Gauge Condition Use Case
Lorenz gauge \( \partial_\mu A^\mu = 0 \) Covariant; leads to wave equations
Coulomb gauge \( \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0 \) Electrostatics + transverse radiation
Temporal gauge \( \phi = 0 \) Hamiltonian formulation
Axial gauge \( A_3 = 0 \) Certain QFT calculations
Note: “Lorenz” (not “Lorentz”)—after Ludvig Lorenz.

5.4 Gauge Fixing & Residual Freedom

  • Even after choosing a gauge (e.g., Lorenz), residual gauge freedom remains:
    • If \( \partial_\mu A^\mu = 0 \), then \( A^\mu \to A^\mu + \partial^\mu \chi \) is allowed iff \( \Box \chi = 0 \)

6. Retarded Potentials

6.1 Motivation

  • Maxwell’s equations are hyperbolic PDEs → disturbances propagate at finite speed \( c \).
  • Solution must respect causality: field at \( (t, \mathbf{r}) \) depends on source at earlier time \( t_{\text{ret}} = t - |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|/c \)

6.2 Derivation in Lorenz Gauge

  • In Lorenz gauge: \( \partial_\mu A^\mu = 0 \)
  • Maxwell’s equations become decoupled wave equations:
    \[ \Box A^\mu = \mu_0 J^\mu \]
  • Green’s function for \( \Box G(x - x') = \delta^{(4)}(x - x') \) is:
    \[ G_{\text{ret}}(x - x') = \frac{\delta\left(t - t' - |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|/c\right)}{4\pi |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|} \]
  • Retarded 4-potential:
    \[ A^\mu(t, \mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{J^\mu\left(t - \frac{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|}{c}, \mathbf{r}'\right)}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|} d^3r' \]
  • Explicitly:
    \[ \phi(t, \mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \int \frac{\rho\left(t_{\text{ret}}, \mathbf{r}'\right)}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|} d^3r' \]
    \[ \mathbf{A}(t, \mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{J}\left(t_{\text{ret}}, \mathbf{r}'\right)}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|} d^3r' \]

6.3 Physical Interpretation

  • Jefimenko’s equations: E and B fields derived from retarded potentials include velocity and acceleration terms → explain radiation from accelerating charges.
  • Causality preserved: No instantaneous action-at-a-distance.
  • Advanced potentials (with \( t + |\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|/c \)) are mathematically valid but non-causal; discarded on physical grounds.

6.4 Liénard–Wiechert Potentials (Point Charge)

For a point charge \( q \) moving on trajectory \( \mathbf{r}_s(t) \):

\[ \phi(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \left( \frac{q}{(1 - \mathbf{n} \cdot \boldsymbol{\beta}) R} \right)_{\text{ret}} \]
\[ \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{\mu_0 c}{4\pi} \left( \frac{q \boldsymbol{\beta}}{(1 - \mathbf{n} \cdot \boldsymbol{\beta}) R} \right)_{\text{ret}} \]

where:

  • \( \boldsymbol{\beta} = \mathbf{v}/c \)
  • \( \mathbf{n} = (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_s)/R \)
  • \( R = |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_s| \)
  • All evaluated at retarded time \( t_{\text{ret}} \)

→ These encode relativistic beaming and radiation reaction.

7. Conservation Laws & Stress-Energy Tensor

7.1 Charge Conservation

  • From \( \partial_\nu (\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu}) = \mu_0 \partial_\nu J^\nu \) and antisymmetry of \( F^{\mu\nu} \):
    \[ \partial_\mu J^\mu = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0 \]

7.2 Electromagnetic Stress-Energy Tensor

\[ T^{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} \left( F^{\mu\alpha} F^\nu_{\ \alpha} - \frac{1}{4} \eta^{\mu\nu} F_{\alpha\beta} F^{\alpha\beta} \right) \]
  • \( T^{00} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \varepsilon_0 E^2 + \frac{B^2}{\mu_0} \right) \) → energy density
  • \( T^{0i} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} (\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{B})_i = S_i / c \) → Poynting vector
  • Conservation: \( \partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = -F^{\nu\alpha} J_\alpha \) → work done on charges

8. Connection to Quantum Theory

  • Gauge invariance → charge conservation (via Noether’s theorem for local U(1) symmetry)
  • Minimal coupling: Replace \( \partial_\mu \to D_\mu = \partial_\mu + i \frac{q}{\hbar} A_\mu \) in quantum wave equations
  • Aharonov–Bohm effect: Physical effect of \( A_\mu \) even where \( F_{\mu\nu} = 0 \) → \( A_\mu \) is more fundamental than E/B in quantum regime

9. Summary: Conceptual Flow

  1. EM is inherently relativistic → unify E and B into \( F^{\mu\nu} \)
  2. Maxwell’s equations → two tensor equations (homogeneous + inhomogeneous)
  3. Gauge freedom → redundancy in \( A^\mu \); choose gauge to simplify
  4. Lorenz gauge → wave equations for \( A^\mu \)
  5. Retarded solutions → enforce causality; fields depend on past light cone
  6. Point charges → Liénard–Wiechert potentials → radiation from acceleration
  7. Stress-energy tensor → local conservation of energy-momentum

10. Common Misconceptions

  • ❌ “Magnetic fields are just relativistic corrections to electric fields.” → No: Both are components of a single geometric object \( F^{\mu\nu} \). What’s “electric” or “magnetic” depends on observer.
  • ❌ “Gauge symmetry is just mathematical convenience.” → No: It’s a fundamental local symmetry with physical consequences (charge quantization, photon masslessness).
  • ❌ “Retarded potentials violate energy conservation.” → No: The full system (charges + fields) conserves energy; radiation carries it away.
