1. Quantum Bits (Qubits)

Definition and Properties of Qubits
  • A qubit is the fundamental unit of quantum information, analogous to classical bits
  • Unlike classical bits (0 or 1), qubits can exist in superposition of both states
  • Key properties: Superposition, Entanglement, Measurement Collapse, No-Cloning Theorem, Unitary Evolution
  • Physical implementations: Electron/nuclear spin, Photon polarization, Superconducting circuits, Trapped ions
  • Represents quantum information through complex probability amplitudes
Superposition Principle and Mathematical Representation
  • Qubit state: |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ where α, β are complex numbers
  • Normalization condition: |α|² + |β|² = 1
  • Probabilistic interpretation: |α|² = probability of measuring 0, |β|² = probability of measuring 1
  • Phase significance: Relative phase between α and β affects quantum interference
  • Example states: |+⟩ = 1/√2(|0⟩ + |1⟩) has 50% probability for each outcome
Bloch Sphere Visualization
  • Geometric representation of all possible single-qubit states on a unit sphere
  • Mathematical mapping: |ψ⟩ = cos(θ/2)|0⟩ + eiφsin(θ/2)|1⟩
  • Coordinates: North pole = |0⟩, South pole = |1⟩, Equator = equal superposition states
  • Angles: θ (polar) determines probabilities, φ (azimuthal) determines phase
  • Measurement bases: X, Y, Z axes correspond to different measurement orientations
Measurement and State Collapse
  • Quantum measurement: Probabilistic collapse to |0⟩ or |1⟩ based on probability amplitudes
  • Irreversibility: Measurement destroys superposition and is non-unitary
  • Basis dependence: Measurement outcomes depend on chosen basis (Z, X, Y, etc.)
  • Expectation values: For observable Z: ⟨Z⟩ = |α|² - |β|²
  • Information loss: Original superposition information is lost after measurement
Comparison with Classical Bits
  • State space: Classical bits have 2 discrete states, qubits have continuous state space
  • Information capacity: Qubits hold infinite potential information but yield only 1 bit per measurement
  • Operations: Classical logic gates vs. reversible unitary quantum gates
  • Copying: Classical bits can be copied, qubits cannot (No-Cloning Theorem)
  • Parallelism: Quantum parallelism through superposition vs. classical sequential processing

2. Quantum Entanglement

Definition and Properties of Entangled States
  • Entanglement is a quantum phenomenon where particles become correlated such that their quantum states cannot be described independently
  • Formally defined as states that cannot be written as product states: |ψ⟩AB ≠ |ϕ⟩A ⊗ |χ⟩B
  • Key properties: Non-separability, Non-locality, Monogamy, Conservation under local unitary operations, Fragility to decoherence
  • Entangled systems are described by a single quantum state even when particles are separated by large distances
  • Individual subsystems do not have definite states on their own when entangled
Bell States and Their Significance
  • Bell states are four maximally entangled two-qubit states forming an orthonormal basis: |Φ⁺⟩, |Φ⁻⟩, |Ψ⁺⟩, |Ψ⁻⟩
  • Each Bell state contains 1 ebit (entanglement bit) of entanglement
  • Form the foundation for quantum protocols including teleportation, superdense coding, and entanglement swapping
  • Violate Bell inequalities, distinguishing quantum mechanics from local hidden-variable theories
  • Act as fundamental resource states in quantum communication and computation
Non-locality and Quantum Correlations
  • Quantum non-locality refers to correlations that cannot be explained by local realistic theories
  • Bell's Theorem proves that quantum mechanics violates constraints imposed by local hidden-variable theories
  • CHSH inequality: Local theories |S| ≤ 2, Quantum mechanics |S| ≤ 2√2 ≈ 2.828 (Tsirelson's bound)
  • No-signaling theorem ensures non-locality cannot be used for faster-than-light communication
  • Experiments confirm violation of Bell inequalities, disproving local realism
Mathematical Representation of Entanglement
  • Schmidt decomposition: |ψ⟩AB = Σi λi |ui⟩A ⊗ |vi⟩B (entangled if Schmidt rank > 1)
  • Positive Partial Transpose (PPT) Criterion: Negative eigenvalues after partial transpose indicate entanglement
  • Entanglement witnesses: Hermitian operators that detect entanglement through negative expectation values
  • Entanglement entropy: S(ρA) = -Tr(ρA log2 ρA) quantifies entanglement for pure states
  • Mixed state entanglement requires more complex criteria than pure states
Applications in Quantum Information Processing
  • Quantum teleportation: Transmits unknown quantum states using classical communication and shared entanglement
  • Superdense coding: Sends two classical bits by transmitting one qubit using pre-shared entanglement
  • Quantum Key Distribution (QKD): E91 protocol uses entanglement and Bell inequality violations for secure communication
  • Quantum computing: Entangling gates (e.g., CNOT) create entanglement essential for quantum speedup
  • Quantum metrology: Entangled states enable Heisenberg-limited precision beyond classical limits

3. Quantum Gates and Circuits

Single-Qubit Gates
  • Pauli gates (X, Y, Z): Fundamental unitary operators representing 180° rotations about x, y, z axes on Bloch sphere
  • Hadamard gate (H): Creates superposition states |+⟩ and |-⟩ from basis states, enables basis change between Z and X
  • Phase gates (S, T): Add complex phases to |1⟩ state; S gate adds +90° phase, T gate adds +45° phase
  • All single-qubit gates correspond to rotations on the Bloch sphere and are represented by 2×2 unitary matrices
  • Single-qubit gates alone are not sufficient for universal quantum computation - require multi-qubit gates
Multi-Qubit Gates
  • CNOT gate: Controlled-NOT operation that flips target qubit if control is |1⟩, essential for creating entanglement
  • Toffoli gate (CCNOT): 3-qubit gate that flips target if both controls are |1⟩, universal for classical reversible computing
  • SWAP gate: Exchanges states of two qubits, can be implemented using three CNOT gates
  • Multi-qubit gates enable conditional operations and entanglement generation between qubits
  • Multi-qubit gates are represented by larger unitary matrices (e.g., CNOT is 4×4 matrix for two-qubit system)
Universal Gate Sets
  • Universal set: A set of gates that can approximate any unitary operation to arbitrary precision
  • Common universal sets: {CNOT, H, T}, {CNOT, single-qubit rotations}, {Toffoli, Hadamard}
  • Non-Clifford gates: At least one non-Clifford gate (like T) is required for universality
  • Gottesman-Knill theorem: Clifford group alone (H, S, CNOT) is not universal for quantum computation
  • Solovay-Kitaev theorem: Guarantees efficient approximation of arbitrary unitaries using universal gate sets
Quantum Circuit Diagrams and Notation
  • Visual representation: Horizontal lines represent qubits, time flows left to right
  • Gate notation: Boxes or symbols applied to qubit lines, multi-qubit gates connect control and target with dots and ⊕
  • Standard symbols: H for Hadamard, ● for control, ⊕ for target (NOT), M for measurement
  • Example circuits: CNOT shown with control and target qubits connected vertically, Toffoli with two controls
  • Classical bits: Often shown as double lines after measurement operations
Quantum Circuit Depth and Complexity
  • Circuit depth: Longest path from input to output, correlates with execution time and noise susceptibility
  • Circuit size: Total number of gates, often counting CNOTs separately due to higher error rates
  • Complexity classes: BQP (Bounded-error Quantum Polynomial time) defines problems solvable with polynomial-size circuits
  • Optimization goals: Minimize CNOT count, reduce depth to stay within coherence time, use gate decomposition
  • Quantum advantage: Often achieved through exponentially smaller depth/size compared to classical algorithms

4. Quantum Algorithms and Complexity

Grover's Search Algorithm
  • Provides quadratic speedup for unstructured search problems, reducing O(N) classical queries to O(√N)
  • Based on amplitude amplification using Grover iteration G = (2|ψ⟩⟨ψ| - I)·Of
  • Starts with uniform superposition and repeatedly applies oracle and diffusion operators
  • Optimal number of iterations is approximately π/4 √N for maximum success probability
  • Applications include database search, optimization problems, and cryptanalysis (reducing effective key length)
Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT)
  • Quantum analog of classical Discrete Fourier Transform, mapping |j⟩ to (1/√N) Σ e2πijk/N |k⟩
  • Efficiently implemented using Hadamard and controlled-phase gates with O(n²) circuit depth
  • Key properties: unitary operation with QFT-1 = QFT†
  • Provides exponential speedup for preparing Fourier states but not for extracting all coefficients
  • Core subroutine in Shor's algorithm, phase estimation, and quantum simulation algorithms
Shor's Algorithm for Integer Factorization
  • Solves integer factorization in polynomial time O((log N)³), providing exponential speedup over classical algorithms
  • Combines classical reduction with quantum period finding subroutine using QFT
  • Factors N by finding order r of random a modulo N, then computing gcd(ar/2 ± 1, N)
  • Uses quantum phase estimation to extract period r from superposition state
  • Significance: breaks RSA cryptography, motivating development of post-quantum cryptography
Quantum Walks
  • Quantum analog of classical random walks with two main types: discrete-time (DTQW) and continuous-time (CTQW)
  • DTQW uses coin and position registers with evolution: |ψ(t+1)⟩ = S·(C⊗I)|ψ(t)⟩
  • Provides quadratic speedup in hitting time compared to classical walks (spreads as t vs. √t)
  • Leverages quantum interference and entanglement between coin and position states
  • Applications include graph search algorithms, graph isomorphism, and quantum simulation of physical processes
Quantum Complexity Classes
  • BQP (Bounded-error Quantum Polynomial Time): problems solvable by quantum computers in polynomial time with error ≤ 1/3
  • BQP contains P and BPP, is contained in PSPACE, and includes factoring and discrete log problems
  • QMA (Quantum Merlin-Arthur): quantum analog of NP where Merlin sends quantum proof to Arthur for verification
  • QMA-complete problem: k-local Hamiltonian problem (quantum analog of SAT)
  • Relationship: BQP ⊆ QMA ⊆ PP, with QMA capturing complexity of quantum many-body problems
