1. Quantum Bits (Qubits)

Definition and Properties of Qubits
  • A qubit is the fundamental unit of quantum information, analogous to classical bits
  • Unlike classical bits (0 or 1), qubits can exist in superposition of both states
  • Key properties: Superposition, Entanglement, Measurement Collapse, No-Cloning Theorem, Unitary Evolution
  • Physical implementations: Electron/nuclear spin, Photon polarization, Superconducting circuits, Trapped ions
  • Represents quantum information through complex probability amplitudes
Superposition Principle and Mathematical Representation
  • Qubit state: |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ where α, β are complex numbers
  • Normalization condition: |α|² + |β|² = 1
  • Probabilistic interpretation: |α|² = probability of measuring 0, |β|² = probability of measuring 1
  • Phase significance: Relative phase between α and β affects quantum interference
  • Example states: |+⟩ = 1/√2(|0⟩ + |1⟩) has 50% probability for each outcome
Bloch Sphere Visualization
  • Geometric representation of all possible single-qubit states on a unit sphere
  • Mathematical mapping: |ψ⟩ = cos(θ/2)|0⟩ + eiφsin(θ/2)|1⟩
  • Coordinates: North pole = |0⟩, South pole = |1⟩, Equator = equal superposition states
  • Angles: θ (polar) determines probabilities, φ (azimuthal) determines phase
  • Measurement bases: X, Y, Z axes correspond to different measurement orientations
Measurement and State Collapse
  • Quantum measurement: Probabilistic collapse to |0⟩ or |1⟩ based on probability amplitudes
  • Irreversibility: Measurement destroys superposition and is non-unitary
  • Basis dependence: Measurement outcomes depend on chosen basis (Z, X, Y, etc.)
  • Expectation values: For observable Z: ⟨Z⟩ = |α|² - |β|²
  • Information loss: Original superposition information is lost after measurement
Comparison with Classical Bits
  • State space: Classical bits have 2 discrete states, qubits have continuous state space
  • Information capacity: Qubits hold infinite potential information but yield only 1 bit per measurement
  • Operations: Classical logic gates vs. reversible unitary quantum gates
  • Copying: Classical bits can be copied, qubits cannot (No-Cloning Theorem)
  • Parallelism: Quantum parallelism through superposition vs. classical sequential processing

2. Quantum Entanglement

Definition and Properties of Entangled States
  • Entanglement is a quantum phenomenon where particles become correlated such that their quantum states cannot be described independently
  • Formally defined as states that cannot be written as product states: |ψ⟩AB ≠ |ϕ⟩A ⊗ |χ⟩B
  • Key properties: Non-separability, Non-locality, Monogamy, Conservation under local unitary operations, Fragility to decoherence
  • Entangled systems are described by a single quantum state even when particles are separated by large distances
  • Individual subsystems do not have definite states on their own when entangled
Bell States and Their Significance
  • Bell states are four maximally entangled two-qubit states forming an orthonormal basis: |Φ⁺⟩, |Φ⁻⟩, |Ψ⁺⟩, |Ψ⁻⟩
  • Each Bell state contains 1 ebit (entanglement bit) of entanglement
  • Form the foundation for quantum protocols including teleportation, superdense coding, and entanglement swapping
  • Violate Bell inequalities, distinguishing quantum mechanics from local hidden-variable theories
  • Act as fundamental resource states in quantum communication and computation
Non-locality and Quantum Correlations
  • Quantum non-locality refers to correlations that cannot be explained by local realistic theories
  • Bell's Theorem proves that quantum mechanics violates constraints imposed by local hidden-variable theories
  • CHSH inequality: Local theories |S| ≤ 2, Quantum mechanics |S| ≤ 2√2 ≈ 2.828 (Tsirelson's bound)
  • No-signaling theorem ensures non-locality cannot be used for faster-than-light communication
  • Experiments confirm violation of Bell inequalities, disproving local realism
Mathematical Representation of Entanglement
  • Schmidt decomposition: |ψ⟩AB = Σi λi |ui⟩A ⊗ |vi⟩B (entangled if Schmidt rank > 1)
  • Positive Partial Transpose (PPT) Criterion: Negative eigenvalues after partial transpose indicate entanglement
  • Entanglement witnesses: Hermitian operators that detect entanglement through negative expectation values
  • Entanglement entropy: S(ρA) = -Tr(ρA log2 ρA) quantifies entanglement for pure states
  • Mixed state entanglement requires more complex criteria than pure states
Applications in Quantum Information Processing
  • Quantum teleportation: Transmits unknown quantum states using classical communication and shared entanglement
  • Superdense coding: Sends two classical bits by transmitting one qubit using pre-shared entanglement
  • Quantum Key Distribution (QKD): E91 protocol uses entanglement and Bell inequality violations for secure communication
  • Quantum computing: Entangling gates (e.g., CNOT) create entanglement essential for quantum speedup
  • Quantum metrology: Entangled states enable Heisenberg-limited precision beyond classical limits
